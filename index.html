    How to read a verdict

    • Positive — added intake is associated with better outcomes
    • Neutral / mixed — little net association, or evidence points both ways
    • Negative — added intake is associated with worse outcomes

    Each card also shows a certainty tier (convincing → probable → limited → inconclusive) and, when expanded, the studies the verdict rests on, the reasoning, and the key caveats. Disagree? Use “Challenge this conclusion.” See The approach for exactly how verdicts are decided.

    Methodology · living document

    How these verdicts are decided

    Nutrition is hard to study: we mostly can’t randomize what people eat for decades, so most evidence is observational — we watch what happens to people who already eat differently. That can mislead. This project’s whole point is to be honest about that: to show the evidence, grade our confidence, and change our mind when the evidence does.

    1. The question we actually answer

    For each food we ask: when this food is regularly added to a typical free-living diet, what tends to happen to long-term health outcomes — mainly all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, and type 2 diabetes? “Free-living” means real people eating normally, not a metabolic ward. Crucially, the answer often depends on what the food replaces (see substitution, below).

    2. What counts as evidence (in priority order)

    1. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) on real health outcomes — strongest, but rare in diet.
    2. Meta-analyses of prospective cohort studies — our main workhorse; pooled long-term follow-up of large populations.
    3. Individual large cohorts — especially with dose-response and substitution analysis.
    4. RCTs on risk markers (LDL, blood pressure, glucose) and mechanism — used as supporting, not decisive, evidence.

    3. The label: positive, negative, or neutral

    We assign a direction from the pooled effect on the outcomes above:

    • Positive — adding the food is consistently associated with lower risk.
    • Negative — consistently associated with higher risk.
    • Neutral / mixed — associations cluster near no effect, are small, or high-quality studies genuinely disagree.

    A verdict is only as good as its certainty tier — a “neutral” backed by conflicting strong studies is very different from a “neutral” backed by two small ones.

    4. The certainty tier

    Adapted from the World Cancer Research Fund / AICR grading and GRADE. It reflects how much we trust the direction, not how big the effect is:

    • Convincing Consistent across many large studies, dose-response, plausible mechanism, and ideally trial support. Unlikely to reverse.
    • Probable Generally consistent good-quality evidence, but with some gaps or unexplained heterogeneity.
    • Limited Suggestive but sparse, inconsistent, or short follow-up.
    • Inconclusive Evidence is genuinely conflicting or too weak to take a side; we say so.

    5. The traps we explicitly account for

    These are why observational nutrition findings so often fail to replicate. Each food card calls out the ones that matter most for it.

    Confounding & the healthy-user effect
    People who eat “healthy” foods differ in dozens of ways (exercise, smoking, income). Good studies adjust for these, but residual confounding always remains — so we discount effects that ride on lifestyle.
    Reverse causation
    Sometimes illness changes diet, not the other way around (e.g. people at risk switch to diet soda). We watch for it and weight studies that exclude early follow-up.
    The substitution problem
    “Adding” a food means something else gives way. Nuts instead of chips ≠ nuts on top of everything. We prefer studies that model substitution, and we name the relevant swap.
    Measurement error
    Diet is usually self-reported via food-frequency questionnaires, which are noisy. This generally blurs real effects toward zero and adds uncertainty.
    Observational ≠ causal
    Association is not proof. We use the Bradford Hill considerations — consistency, strength, dose-response, plausibility, and (where it exists) experimental evidence — to judge how causal a pattern looks.

    6. How verdicts change — and how you can challenge them

    This is a living document. Conclusions are dated, versioned, and meant to be revised as evidence accrues or when someone shows our reasoning is wrong. Every food card has a “Challenge this conclusion” button that opens a structured issue where you can point to a study we missed, a bias we underweighted, or a better substitution model.

    A challenge is acted on when it shows one of:

    • A study (or studies) we didn’t account for that shifts the pooled picture;
    • A methodological flaw in how we weighed the existing evidence;
    • New higher-tier evidence (e.g. a trial) that supersedes older cohorts.

    When a verdict changes, the change is recorded in that food’s revision log, visible on the card.

    7. Honest limitations

    • The food list is curated and not exhaustive.
    • Study selection still involves judgement; we cite sources so you can check us.
    • Verdicts are population averages — individuals (age, genetics, conditions) can differ a lot.
    • This is not medical or dietary advice.

    Methodology and sources are also documented in METHODOLOGY.md in the repository, with the full research notes behind this approach.
