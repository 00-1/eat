    Methodology · living document

    How these verdicts are decided

    Nutrition is hard to study: we mostly can’t randomize what people eat for decades, so most evidence is observational — we watch what happens to people who already eat differently. That can mislead. This project’s whole point is to be honest about that: to show the evidence, grade our confidence, and change our mind when the evidence does.

    1. The question we actually answer

    For each food we ask: when this food is regularly added to a typical free-living diet, what tends to happen to long-term health outcomes — mainly all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, and type 2 diabetes? “Free-living” means real people eating normally, not a metabolic ward. Crucially, the answer often depends on what the food replaces (see substitution, below).

    2. What counts as evidence (in priority order)

    1. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) on real health outcomes — strongest, but rare in diet.
    2. Meta-analyses of prospective cohort studies — our main workhorse; pooled long-term follow-up of large populations.
    3. Individual large cohorts — especially with dose-response and substitution analysis.
    4. RCTs on risk markers (LDL, blood pressure, glucose) and mechanism — used as supporting, not decisive, evidence.

    3. The label: positive, negative, or neutral

    Direction comes from the conservatively-estimated association — the approach the IHME/GBD Burden of Proof method uses: take the closest-to-null effect the evidence reasonably supports (after accounting for how much studies disagree), and only call a direction if its uncertainty interval excludes “no effect.”

    • Positive — the conservative estimate shows lower risk, consistently, with the interval excluding no-effect.
    • Negative — same, but higher risk.
    • Neutral / mixed — the conservative interval crosses no-effect, the effect is trivially small, or high-quality studies genuinely disagree.

    This makes neutral the default: it is harder to earn a directional label than to stay neutral.

    4. The certainty tier

    We rate certainty on the NutriGrade scale, built specifically to grade meta-analyses of cohort studies. It scores nine things — risk of bias, precision, heterogeneity, directness, publication and funding bias, study design, effect size, and dose-response — and lands in one of four tiers:

    • High Consistent across many good-quality studies, dose-response, plausible mechanism, ideally trial support. Unlikely to reverse.
    • Moderate Generally consistent good-quality evidence, with gaps or some unexplained heterogeneity.
    • Low Suggestive but sparse, inconsistent, or short follow-up.
    • Very low Genuinely conflicting or too weak to take a side; we say so.

    To keep this reproducible, the tier is computed, not assigned. For each food we record objective evidence facts (pooled relative risk, sample size, heterogeneity, study designs, funding, …); a fixed function scores eight dimensions 0–2 (max 16) and the total sets the tier. Our judgement lives in the rules, applied identically to every food — not in any single number. Expand a card to see the computed scores and the conservative estimate.

    • Study quality / confounding control · Consistency · Precision · Directness
    • Effect size · Dose-response · Freedom from publication/funding bias · Experimental or mechanistic corroboration
    • High 13–16 (≥80%)
    • Moderate 10–12 (≥60%)
    • Low 7–9 (≥40%)
    • Very low 0–6 (<40%)

    This is our transparent adaptation of NutriGrade applied as a rubric — not a recomputation of an official published score. Where an official NutriGrade score or GBD Burden-of-Proof star rating exists for a food–outcome pair, we defer to it.

    What kind of evidence carries it

    Two foods can land on the same tier for very different reasons, so each card also shows an evidence-basis chip, derived from the same scores:

    • Convergent cohort data and causal/trial evidence agree
    • Observation-led carried by direct cohort outcome data
    • Mechanism/trial-led rests on a validated causal pathway, not large cohorts
    • Limited / contested neither route is strong — held cautiously

    This is also where the guiding rule lives: mechanism corroborates, it never overrides. Direction comes from what is observed when a food is added to real diets; a biomarker alone (a blood-sugar spike, a cholesterol bump) never convicts a food when the outcome data says otherwise. That’s why carb-heavy whole foods stay positive and coconut oil stays neutral — but trans fat, where the causal pathway is validated and cohorts agree, earns high certainty.

    5. The rule that ties it together

    No published source prescribes the final combined rule, so this one is ours — stated plainly so you can challenge it:

    1. Default to neutral.
    2. Assign positive/negative only if the conservative estimate excludes no-effect in a consistent direction and certainty is at least Moderate.
    3. A real-looking effect with only Low / Very-low certainty stays neutral (leaning …), noted in the reasoning — not a hard verdict.
    4. Foods affecting several outcomes: the verdict follows the net direction across priority outcomes (all-cause mortality first); reported certainty is the lowest tier among the outcomes driving it.
    5. When grades disagree, take the more conservative one.

    One honest caveat baked into this: the Burden-of-Proof score blends effect size with evidence strength, so a small-but-certain effect scores low. We treat magnitude and certainty as separate things, and the method itself is contested.

    6. The traps we explicitly account for

    These are why observational nutrition findings so often fail to replicate. Each food card calls out the ones that matter most for it.

    Confounding & the healthy-user effect
    People who eat “healthy” foods differ in dozens of ways (exercise, smoking, income). Good studies adjust for these, but residual confounding always remains — so we discount effects that ride on lifestyle.
    Reverse causation
    Sometimes illness changes diet, not the other way around (e.g. people at risk switch to diet soda). We watch for it and weight studies that exclude early follow-up.
    The substitution problem
    “Adding” a food means something else gives way. Nuts instead of chips ≠ nuts on top of everything. We prefer studies that model substitution and we name the relevant swap — but cautiously: a 2026 review found 53% of substitution-modeling studies used unvalidated food-frequency-questionnaire inputs, so we treat substitution ambiguity as confidence-lowering, not solved.
    Measurement error
    Diet is usually self-reported via food-frequency questionnaires, which are noisy. This generally blurs real effects toward zero and adds uncertainty.
    Observational ≠ causal
    Association is not proof. We use the Bradford Hill considerations — consistency, strength, dose-response, plausibility, and (where it exists) experimental evidence — to judge how causal a pattern looks.

    7. How verdicts change — and how you can challenge them

    This is a living document. Conclusions are dated, versioned, and meant to be revised as evidence accrues or when someone shows our reasoning is wrong — a study we missed, a bias we underweighted, a better substitution model, or a sub-score that should change. Challenges are handled by the maintainer directly: each is reviewed with AI-assisted research and the verdict is updated if it holds up.

    A challenge is acted on when it shows one of:

    • A study (or studies) we didn’t account for that shifts the pooled picture;
    • A methodological flaw in how we weighed the existing evidence;
    • New higher-tier evidence (e.g. a trial) that supersedes older cohorts.

    When a verdict changes, the change is recorded in that food’s revision log, visible on the card.

    8. Honest limitations

    • The food list is curated and not exhaustive.
    • Study selection still involves judgement; we cite sources so you can check us.
    • Verdicts are population averages — individuals (age, genetics, conditions) can differ a lot.
    • This is not medical or dietary advice.
