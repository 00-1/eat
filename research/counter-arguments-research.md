# Research: real counter-arguments & the model-level critiques

Source research behind the per-food **Steelmanning attempts** and the Approach
tab's **"Criticisms of this approach."** Produced by a fact-checked, multi-source
pass (fan-out search → fetch → 3-vote adversarial verification → synthesis):
9 verified findings, 27 sources. _Last run: 2026-06-29._

## Bottom line

Most contrarian "this food is secretly bad/good" claims rest on **mechanism /
biomarker reductionism** that does not survive contact with outcome data — but
several carry **genuine merit** or expose **real limitations** of the
observational model. The honest stance: judge foods by long-term outcomes on real
diets, treat acute biomarker arguments as hypothesis-generating, and apply
skepticism **symmetrically** (the biases that dismiss harm signals also dismantle
the alcohol "benefit").

## Verified findings

1. **Fruit "sugar spikes" — refuted at outcome level.** Meta-analysis of **19 RCTs
   (888 diabetics)**: fruit *lowered* fasting glucose (−8.38 mg/dL, 95% CI −12.34
   to −4.43) with neutral HbA1c. RCT-level, directly contradicts carnivore/low-carb
   framing. _(Ren et al., Front Endocrinol 2023, PMC10198260.)_
2. **Red meat "unfairly maligned" (NutriRECS) — genuinely grounded but weak.**
   Johnston et al. issued a weak/low-certainty recommendation; absolute effect ~8
   deaths/1,000 (CI includes 0) per 3 servings/wk over 11y. Exposes the
   low-certainty limitation — but had an undisclosed industry COI. _(Ann Intern
   Med 2019, 10.7326/M19-1621.)_
3. **The "red meat is fine, trials show it" defense is funding-biased.** In 44
   trials, 66% had industry links and industry-linked studies were **~4× more
   likely** to report favorable/neutral results (OR 3.75, 95% CI 1.62–8.67).
   _(López-Moreno et al., AJCN 2025.)_
4. **Full-fat dairy partly vindicated.** PURE (136,384 people): >2 servings/day
   total dairy → lower mortality+CVD (HR 0.83), major CVD (0.78), stroke (0.66);
   whole-fat subgroup not worse. Observational. _(Dehghan et al., Lancet 2018.)_
5. **The "food-matrix → SFA limits are wrong" position is real but overstated.**
   A named scientific position (Astrup, Mozaffarian, Krauss et al., JACC 2020) with
   partial outcome support for full-fat dairy/cheese — but the stronger "SFA limits
   unsupported" claims were *refuted* here, and the paper had dairy-industry ties.
6. **Coconut oil "superfood" — refuted.** AHA: coconut oil raises LDL "with no
   known offsetting favorable effects," advises against it; replacing SFA with PUFA
   cuts CVD ~30%. _(Sacks et al., AHA Presidential Advisory, Circulation 2017.)_
7. **Moderate-alcohol J-curve — an artifact.** 107 cohorts (~4.8M): benefit
   vanished in higher-quality studies (RR 0.98, NS), appeared only in low-quality
   ones, and was >1.0 in nonsmoker cohorts — abstainer/"sick-quitter" bias.
   _(Stockwell/Zhao et al., J Stud Alcohol Drugs 2024.)_
8. **Industry-funded skepticism is real (and weaponized).** Coca-Cola's Global
   Energy Balance Network pushed "inactivity, not sugar"; FOI of 18,030 emails
   showed deliberate obscuring of funding. Validates distrust of single studies —
   in both directions. _(Serodio et al., Public Health Nutr 2020; PMC10200649.)_
9. **Artificial-sweetener microbiome harm — real position, weak human evidence.**
   Suez et al. (Nature 2014) showed glucose intolerance via microbiota in mice; the
   human bit was n=7, and a 2021 study failed to replicate in healthy humans. The
   causal-transfer framing was *refuted* here.

## Refuted (did not survive verification)

- "SFA-rich matrix foods are *not* associated with CVD, so total SFA doesn't
  matter" — overreach beyond dairy/cheese.
- "Most meta-analyses find no benefit from reducing SFA" — not supported as stated.
- "Sweetener harm is causal/microbiome-transferable in humans" — mouse-level;
  human replication failed.
